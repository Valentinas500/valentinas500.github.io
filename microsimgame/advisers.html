What do your advisers have to say?


I believe in the markets. If Absurdistan’s clothing industry is declining, it is probably because we do not have a comparative advantage. Helping an industry in decline will probably only delay the inevitable. Also, if help them, does that mean we have to help other failing industries, or individual businesses? It is not the government’s job to get involved in failing businesses. Let the markets decide.

Even if we did pour a lot of resources to help the clothing industry, it may be completely ineffective. It may also require increasing public debt and bureaucracy. I say, we let unprofitable businesses go bankrupt and people lose jobs. Over time, capital will flow to other areas and workers will find new jobs and all will go back to normal. The transition for some may be hard but so is life for all. If By doing nothing, at least we will not increase public debt.


This may seem unconventional but hear me out! If an industry is in decline, we may have a comparative disadvantage. Why can we not speed the transition away from industries that we do not have a comparative advantage in? I suggest we use export tariffs. An export tariff is a tax on domestic goods being shipped abroad. It would raise the price of domestic clothing abroad and most surely reduce our exports. By implementing export tariffs, we will definitely hurt our exports and the industry as a whole but since it is going to inevitably fail, this will only speed the process up. Moreover, it may further reduce clothing prices at home.

The extra revenues from export tariffs can then be used either to fund education or reduce public debt. Anyway, I suggest we profit from this situation. If the industry is declining anyway, I don’t think it is unethical to do so. However, it is up to you to decide, not me.


An import tariff is a tax on goods and services imported into Absurdistan. It will increase the price of foreign clothing and thus give a price advantage to locally-produced goods over similar goods which are imported. Moreover, they raise revenues for governments. It is a pretty standard practice: most countries impose tariff on most imported products.

An alternative option is to impose an import quota. A quota is a physical limitation on the quantity of a good that can be imported. A simple quota would not increase government’s revenues but this can easily be fixed by auctioning off import quotas.

Also, sometimes other countries are subsidizing their domestic industries creating uneven playing field. How can our companies compete with foreigners if they are being helped by their governments!? Other times, some companies are dumping their proudcts here to bankrupt our local industry. Trade barriers will put a stop to all of it and make the playing field level again.

You may have not heard of dumping. It is when an exporting firm sets a lower price in an export market than in their own. For example, neighboring country’s Vance Refrigeration sells Fridge 1000 for $500 in their local market but exports it to us for $300. See more here: https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dumping.asp.

Check our US tariff profile compiled by the World Trade Organization (WTO): https://www.wto.org/english/res_e/statis_e/daily_update_e/tariff_profiles/US_e.pdf. As you can see, US, as most other countries, applies tariffs to imports from most trading partners.

Here you can find the evolution of weighed average applied tariff rates in the US and other countries since 1988: https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/TM.TAX.MRCH.WM.AR.ZS.

If the domestic clothing industry is declining, government programs can help facilitate employment through public training programs. They aim to ease workers’ transition from declining to growing industries and to help disadvantaged groups escape poverty. Advocates of these programs believe that they make the economy operate more efficiently by keeping the labor force more fully employed and that they reduce the inequities inherent in a constantly changing market economy.

However, critics of these programs question whether the government should get involved with the process of training workers. They argue that it is better to let the private market match workers and jobs and that the government is no better—and most likely worse—at deciding what kinds of worker training would be most valuable.

Look at the U.S. Department of Labor’s Employment and Training Administration (ETA) that provides information on training programs in the US: https://www.dol.gov/general/topic/training/adulttraining.

I would also ask if the costs of such adult education/training programs are high? Would the government not spend a lot of money for programs no one wants? It is up to you to decide.


Nationalization is a government take-over of a select company or selected companies. After nationalization, state appointed officials can make calls about all the business operations. However, government run companies typically lack the profit motive, and thus are run inefficient. On the other hand, there are some potential benefits of nationalization. Among them, for example, the government has access to cheap credit which could allow for necessary investment at low cost. Can the government run the nationalized clothing company in a way that is profitable in an increasingly competitive world clothing market? If not, taking this on the government may mean long lasting losses and a bleak future.

There are somexample of governments nationalizing oil, rail, or mining industries. For example, German railway company Deutsche Bahn is owned by the government. Many of the Latin American oil companies are also owned by national governments.

Take a look at a quick video explaining nationalization here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_k2kOYBGoU0.

There is an ongoing discussion about nationalization of water industry in the UK. Take a look at the discussion here: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=F9mouLsQQKE. Also, look at the technical report by Prof. David Hall about how beneficial nationalizing this industry would be in the UK: https://gala.gre.ac.uk/id/eprint/17277/.
