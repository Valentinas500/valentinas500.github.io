Some costs, called fixed costs, do not vary with the quantity of output produced. They are incurred even if the firm produces nothing at all. This includes maintaining the factory no matter how little we produce. The larger teh factory, the larger the fixed costs are.

Among our main competitors, total fixed costs are between $480.16 and $513.83.


When long-run average total cost declines as output increases, there are said to be economies of scale. This typically happens when our factory gets bigger. In a larger factory, workers can divide between the tasks they do, and get better (or more specialized) at the one or two task they decide to take upon themselves. Check out this cool website that shows what economist Adam Smith noticed about workers in two different pin factories: https://www.adamsmithworks.org/pin_factory.html. As you will learn, division of labor allows higher productivity. We can produce more with the same resources. Amazing!

Currently in the market, I believe that an average firm is still in the range of economies of scale. Higher factory capacity will most likely allow to reduce average total costs.


It is important to understand factory capacity utilization. It refers to how much of the total possible output we are actualy producing. If we have a large factory running but producing only a few items, while most of the factory is running idle, we incur large costs. In other words, for every box that we produce we have high marginal costs. However, as we increase factory utilization, marginal costs and so do average total costs decrease. After reaching around 90 percent, marginal costs start to increase. Check out this short video about marginal costs to refresh your memory: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ualnn_YTMfI.

My simple estimates suggest that average total costs may be two times higher when we are running the company at 30 percent compared to 90 percent capacity.


Because of the law of demand, a fall in the price of a good raises the quantity demanded. The price elasticity of demand measures how much the quantity demanded responds to a change in price. We call the demand for a good elastic if the quantity demanded responds substantially to changes in the price. Demand is said to be inelastic if the quantity demanded responds only slightly to changes in the price. The price elasticity of demand simply measures how willing consumers are to buy less of the good as its price rises.

To find out how consumers respond to a change in price, try adjusting the price of your product slightly but remember you won’t be able to learn anything from price adjustments if any changes in the market occur.
